Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

Generative Deep Learning Machine Learning Probability
TL;DR

A VAE is a neural network that learns to compress data into a small probabilistic "latent space," and then reconstruct it. Because the latent space is probabilistic, you can sample new points from it and decode them into novel outputs — making VAEs one of the earliest and simplest deep generative models.

Overview

Imagine you have 60,000 pictures of handwritten digits. A VAE tries to learn two things at once:

  1. An encoder that turns each image into a short list of numbers (say, 20 numbers instead of 784 pixels). This short list is called the latent vector.
  2. A decoder that turns that short list back into an image.

That much is just an autoencoder. What makes a VAE variational is this: instead of the encoder producing a single latent vector, it produces a probability distribution over possible latent vectors — typically a Gaussian with a learned mean and variance. Training also regularizes that distribution to stay close to a standard normal N(0, I).

Two consequences fall out of this:

  • The latent space becomes smooth and continuous — nearby points decode to similar-looking outputs.
  • You can sample a random latent vector from N(0, I), run it through the decoder, and get a plausible new image the model has never seen.

How It Works (Visual)

The Two Forces That Train a VAE

VAEs are trained by minimizing a loss with two opposing terms:

Loss = Reconstruction error + β · KL divergence
Reconstruction error
"Did the decoder's output look like the original input?" Usually mean-squared error for images or cross-entropy for binary data. Pushes the network to encode useful detail.
KL divergence
"Is the encoder's distribution close to a standard normal?" Measured by KL( q(z|x) || N(0, I) ). Pushes the latent space to be smooth, centered, and samplable. Without this, a VAE collapses into a regular autoencoder.

These two pull in opposite directions: the reconstruction term wants the encoder to be precise (use the whole latent space), while the KL term wants it to stay tight around zero. The compromise is what gives VAEs their generative ability.

The reparameterization trick. You can't backpropagate through a random sample directly. The trick: instead of sampling z ~ N(μ, σ²), write z = μ + σ · ε where ε ~ N(0, 1). Now the randomness is an input, and gradients flow cleanly through μ and σ.

Real-World Examples

Molecular design

Encode molecules into a continuous latent space, then search or optimize within that space to find novel compounds with desired properties (e.g. drug candidates).

Anomaly detection

Train a VAE on "normal" data (healthy ECGs, clean server logs). When something unusual appears, the reconstruction error spikes — a signal that something's off.

Face / image generation

Sample from the latent prior and decode to get new synthetic faces. Interpolating between two latents morphs smoothly from one face to another.

Engineering design

Parameterize complex geometries (airfoils, trusses, topology layouts) as latent vectors. Optimization over a smooth latent space is easier than over raw geometry.

Representation learning

Use the encoder as a feature extractor. The latent vector is a compact summary that can feed into classifiers or downstream models.

Data imputation

If pixels or sensor readings are missing, the VAE can fill them in plausibly — useful in medical imaging and sensor networks.

The Math (Lightly)

A VAE maximizes a quantity called the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

ELBO(x) = E_{q(z|x)}[ log p(x | z) ] − KL( q(z|x) || p(z) )

Intuitively: the first term is "how well does the decoder reconstruct x from a z drawn by the encoder?" The second is "how far is the encoder's distribution from the prior?" Maximizing the ELBO is equivalent to minimizing the loss above.

It's called a lower bound because maximizing it also maximizes (a bound on) the true likelihood log p(x) — which is generally intractable to compute directly.

Why Papers Care

  • VAEs gave a clean, principled way to do deep generative modeling — encoder, decoder, and a probabilistic story that ties them together.
  • The latent space is interpretable and interpolatable. Unlike GANs, you can embed real data points and move around.
  • They're foundational: diffusion models, normalizing flows, and modern latent-variable systems all borrow intuitions from VAEs.

Related Terms
